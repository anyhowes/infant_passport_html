Nutrition

Breastfeeding/formula

During the first six months of life, breastfeeding is the only nutrition that should typically be necessary although it is reccomended that breastfed babies receive vitamin D supplements of 400 IU per day. If, however, you choose commercial infant formula as an alternative, vitamin D supplementation is not necessary. Exclusive breastfeeding during the first six months of life is the accepted standard of nutrition as recommended by the World Health Organization (WHO), and is an important part of healthy infant growth and development. Breastmilk provides infants with the correct quality and quantity of nutrients in a form that is easy to digest and absorb. In addition, breastmilk contains critical bio-active agents including anti-infective immunoglobulins, as well as factors that stimulate the development of the small intestine and aid in the digestion and absorption of nutrients.

Six months of exclusive breastfeeding is sufficient to allow children to develop to the point where they are ready for nutritionally dense solid foods. Continue to breastfeed as long as the mother is comfortable up until around 9 months of age at which point one can transition to cow's milk as the main milk source. Keep in mind that only 3.25% M.F. should be consumed until 2 years of age when partially skimmed milk can be introduced. Remember that rice or nut beverages should never be used as an infant's main milk source.
